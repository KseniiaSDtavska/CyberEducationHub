CYBERSECURITY BASIC

There are many ways to protect your information online. Some of the basic cybersecurity principles you should have already implemented include using strong passwords, enabling multi-factor authentication (MFA), updating software regularly, limiting location access, not sharing too much information on social media, backing up your data, and using a VPN.

Keep reading to learn more about how you can implement these basic cybersecurity principles.

1 -- Use strong and unique passwords.

One of the first steps to make sure you're implementing cybersecurity best practices is to create strong and unique passwords for all of your accounts. A strong and unique password is long and complex, meaning it is at least 16 characters long and contains a combination of uppercase and lowercase letters, numbers, and special characters. It is important to remember that these passwords should not include consecutive numbers, birthdays, common words and phrases, personal information and, most importantly, should not be reused for multiple accounts. It's important to keep track of your passwords and store them in a secure manner, so consider using a password manager. A password manager is a tool that allows users to safely and securely store and manage passwords for all of their applications and services. When using a password manager, the only password you must remember is the master password.

2 -- Enable multi-factor authentication.

Once all of your accounts have strong passwords, you should also enable multi-factor authentication (MFA) for them if possible. The purpose of multi-factor authentication is to provide your accounts with an additional layer of security by requiring one or more forms of authentication to gain access to your account. Multi-factor authentication provides excellent protection against account takeover because if an attacker is somehow able to compromise your password, they will still not be able to log into your account without providing additional authentication factors. Common methods of multi-factor authentication include using an authentication app or biometric authentication such as Face ID.

3 -- Update your software regularly.

One cybersecurity best practice that many people procrastinate on is updating all of their software. Software updates not only improve features and add new ones, but also address security flaws, fix bugs, and improve your device's performance. If you don't update your device to the latest software version, it can reduce its performance and make it vulnerable to cyberattacks. When software is not updated immediately, it opens a loophole that attackers can use to more easily infect your devices with malicious software known as malware. When malware is installed, it can spy on you and collect your sensitive data that attackers can use for their malicious purposes.

4 -- Restrict access to location information in apps and browsers

A cybersecurity and privacy risk issue that is often overlooked is allowing location information to be shared in apps and browsers. While this feature can be useful, it is also a risk because it leaves you vulnerable to attacks such as location data leakage. Location leaks occur when attackers track a victim's location and use that information to compromise their privacy. This can be dangerous to both your privacy and your cybersecurity in general, as this information can be used to discover your technology and tamper with your devices. Additionally, attackers can use your location to publish your information, which can lead to attacks such as online bullying, false emergency calls, identity theft, physical assault or stalking.

5 -- Over-sharing your information on social media sites

Attackers often use personal information collected on social media to launch cyberattacks. Common attacks that happen to those who share excessive information on social media can include account takeover, social engineering, and even physical attacks. Therefore, it is important not to share too much information or reveal personal details about your online life to reduce the attack surface. Some examples of over-sharing are frequently showing your location in photos and regularly publishing the identities of your friends and family.

6 -- Back up your data

Data is vulnerable and can be lost in an instant due to hacking, user error, corrupted files, or even simple interference on your part. Not having a plan to restore your information, such as a backup, can result in you losing that information forever. Backing up your data to a safe location online ensures that even in the worst case, you will never lose it.

7 -- Use a VPN

A Virtual Private Network (VPN) provides you with a secure connection by managing your internet connection through a private carrier rather than your regular internet service provider (ISP). A VPN masks your IP address and encrypts the data you expect and operates at all times. When using public Wi-Fi, which can be intercepted by malicious users, a VPN provides an extra layer of security and privacy within the public network, allowing you to connect safely. A VPN can also help prevent bandwidth throttling, where your ISP intends to reduce your internet speed.
