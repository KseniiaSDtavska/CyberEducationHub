WHAT IS SMISHING




Phishing is a social engineering technique that attackers use to steal user data or compromise corporate information, most often via email. Phishing attacks are most effective when users are unaware of them. Types of phishing There are many types of phishing attacks. These include classic email attacks, social media attacks, and attacks whose names are made up of different words, such as smishing and vishing.
• - Phishing - typically a scam via email
• - Targeted phishing - emails designed to defraud specific people
• - Wailing - emails usually targeted at senior executives
• - Internal phishing - phishing emails are forwarded internally within an organisation
• - Vishing - scammers call their victims
• - Smishing - scammers send phishing SMS messages
• - Phishing via social media - scammers post phishing posts
• - Pharming - compromising the DNS cache
Insider phishing Internal phishing attacks are a growing concern. They occur when a trusted user sends a phishing email to an employee in their organisation. Since the sender is not suspicious, recipients are more likely to click on the link, open the attachment, or share the requested information. To send internal phishing emails, an attacker controls your email account with compromised credentials. The attacker can also gain control of your device either physically - if they find or steal it - or by installing malware on the device. Internal phishing emails are usually part of multi-stage attacks with the ultimate goal of either ransomware extortion or theft of financial assets and intellectual property.


Smishing is a type of phishing attack that involves mobile devices. Since they are now more popular than PCs, attackers could not ignore them. In a smishing attack, you receive a text message on your phone about a problem with your account and a phone number to call to resolve the issue. Once you call, you will speak either personally to the abuser or to their ‘employee’ hired to continue the scam. If you don't call back, the attackers may call you themselves and tell you that your account has been attacked and you need to provide information on it to resolve the problem. The more return calls you receive, the more likely the hackers are to succeed. Such an attack is called vishing. Phishing via social media Social media has become such an important part of our lives that hackers can easily use it for phishing scams. One common Facebook phishing scheme is to post ‘deals’ or ‘offers’ on friends' accounts with instructions to click on the link. To pull off this scam, attackers must gain access to the account. If some company's online servers have leaked passwords, it can help hackers gain access to multiple accounts. They try entering the same email and password combinations on other common platforms such as Facebook or LinkedIn.
