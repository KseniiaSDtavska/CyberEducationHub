Dipfake technology is no longer limited to entertainment or digital content creation. It has become a powerful tool in the hands of fraudsters who use fake videos and voices to deceive people and steal funds. Let's explore in this article how modern algorithms work, why they are so difficult to detect, and what technologies help to counter the threat.
What dipfakes are and how they evolved.
Dipfakes is a technology based on artificial intelligence algorithms that allows you to create fake images, video and audio recordings. The essence of the technology is the use of deep neural networks to replace the face, voice or even facial expressions of one person with the data of another, creating the illusion of authenticity. The principles of dipfakes are based on the use of generative-adversarial networks. This method involves two neural networks: one (the generator) creates fake content and the other (the discriminator) tries to determine whether the content is real or fake. For video generation, a process is used where the original face is replaced by another face, preserving the expressions of emotion and lip movements.


Voice spoofing uses algorithms that analyse sound patterns, tones, and accents to reproduce another person's voice. Modern AI models allow for real-time voice synthesis, making dipfakes particularly dangerous in the context of fraud. Today, dipfeaks are used not only in the entertainment industry, but also in criminal schemes, which emphasizes the importance of studying this technology and developing methods of protection.
Fraud and Dipfakes: New Threats
As technology advances, dipfakes are becoming an increasingly powerful tool in the fraudster's arsenal. The ability to spoof video and audio in real time opens new horizons for criminal schemes, making them more convincing and harder to recognize. One of the most well-known examples of the use of dipfakes in crime involves voice spoofing. Fraudsters imitate the voices of company executives and request large sums of money through phone calls. Another threat is videos and mugshots in messengers, where the faces of people you know are faked. For example, fraudsters send videos or make video calls on behalf of a relative or boss, asking for financial assistance. Such techniques are used not only to extort money, but also to spread misinformation. Dipfakes also find use in blackmail. Criminals create compromising videos by combining the victim's real data with fake content and threaten to publish them if the demand is not met. Today's dipfakes are already making it much more difficult for even experienced users to identify fakes, which requires the development of new defence technologies and increased threat awareness.


Technologies against dipfakes: is there a defence?
With the proliferation of dipfakes, there is a need to develop effective technologies to recognise them. Current tools are already capable of analysing audio and video content for spoofs, although they are still far from perfect. One approach to detecting dipfakes is algorithms that analyse video microdetails. For example, they can detect inconsistencies in shadows, artefacts at object boundaries or unrealistic behaviour of facial muscles. Some technologies analyse eye movements or blink rates, as fakes often fail to capture these nuances. Audio dipfakes look for deviations in voice frequencies or suspiciously flat intonation. Algorithms can detect characteristic ‘digital fingerprints’ left by audio generators and compare them with original recordings. Major technology companies are developing their own solutions for detecting dipfakes. For example, Microsoft has created a tool called Video Authenticator, which assesses the likelihood of counterfeiting by analysing each frame of a video.



Tools that previously required significant computing resources are now available to virtually any user. Fraudsters are quickly adopting specialised platforms and open source libraries such as DeepFaceLab or FakeApp, adapting them for their own purposes. The emergence of artificial intelligence models that can learn in a matter of hours allows attackers to quickly customise algorithms for specific tasks.
The Future of Dipfakes: Challenges and Solutions
Dipfake technologies continue to evolve rapidly, presenting both tremendous opportunities and serious threats. In the future, video and audio generation algorithms will become even more accurate, faster, and more affordable. This will lead to the creation of fakes that will be nearly impossible to distinguish from reality with the naked eye. At the same time, the development of machine learning may open the way to new types of attacks where dipfakes will be used in real time, completely mimicking the manner of speech and human behaviour. To protect against such threats, it is important to understand not only the technology but also your vulnerabilities. Personal vigilance remains key: checking sources, double-checking facts and avoiding the transmission of sensitive information through unreliable channels. It is also important to minimise the amount of personal data published online, as photos, videos and audio recordings are often the basis for creating fake content.
